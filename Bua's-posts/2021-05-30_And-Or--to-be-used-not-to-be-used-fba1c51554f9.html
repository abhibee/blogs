And/Or, to be used/not to be used

And/or (also and or) is a grammatical conjunction used to indicate that one or more of the cases it connects may occur. It is used as an inclusive or, which means it allows both possibilities as well as either of them. So, if either A or B is True, or if both are True, then the statement value is True. (as in logic and mathematics). In spoken language and or might be inclusive or exclusive(Exclusive OR only allows one possibility. So if either A or B is true, then and only then is the value True).

The use of the term and/or is ubiquitous in legal language. Lawyers use it all the time in legal contexts which includes statutes, contracts, and pleadings. However beginning in the 1930s, many judges decided that the term and/or should never be used in legal drafting. The phrase has been termed as vague and senseless. The phrase has come under criticism in both American and British courts. Judges have often called it a "freakish fad", “ Janus-faced verbal monstrosity", a “ crutch for sloppy thinkers" an "accuracy-destroying symbol", and "meaningless".

Today also many critics still maintain that the phrase and/or is inherently ambiguous and should be avoided whenever possible .

And/or, however, is not ambiguous at all. It has a clear and concise meaning. The problem is with its usage. It is criticised mainly because of the inability of people using it. When used properly it has a definite meaning . The construct means “A or B or both.”

The areas such as Pleadings, contracts, statutes, and patent claims allow for a logical use of and/or.

Whereas, some legal areas — such as jury instructions, search warrants, and jury verdicts — do not typically allow a drafter to provide options, making and/or unsuitable.

Despite the few contexts in which and/or should be avoided, the construct should not be discarded simply because individuals occasionally misuse the term. It should be used with proper caution and care .
